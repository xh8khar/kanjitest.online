One of the most common questions Japanese learners ask is: **how many kanji do I need to know for each JLPT level?** The answer varies significantly from N5 to N1, and knowing the exact count helps you plan your study schedule and track your progress.

Here is the complete breakdown of kanji counts for every JLPT level, from beginner to advanced.

## Kanji Count by JLPT Level

| Level | Kanji Count | Cumulative Total | Approximate Study Hours |
|-------|------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| N5 | 79 | 79 | 150–250 |
| N4 | 168 | 247 | 300–500 |
| N3 | 365 | 612 | 600–900 |
| N2 | 686 | 1,298 | 1,100–1,600 |
| N1 | 1,237 | 2,535 | 2,000+ |

Each level builds on the previous one, so you need to know all kanji from lower levels before tackling the next.

## JLPT N5: 79 Kanji

N5 is the entry-level exam. The 79 kanji cover the most fundamental characters — numbers, basic directions, time expressions, and simple everyday concepts like 人 (person), 山 (mountain), and 水 (water).

These kanji typically have 1–2 readings each. Many learners master N5 kanji in 2–3 months of consistent study using tools like [N5 flashcards](/n5/flashcards/) and [N5 study pages](/n5/study/).

**Study tip:** Focus on reading recognition first. Can you see the character and immediately recall its meaning? Use the [N5 practice tests](/n5/sets/1/) to test yourself.

## JLPT N4: 168 Kanji (247 cumulative)

N4 adds 168 new kanji to the 79 from N5, bringing the total to 247. These characters appear in everyday situations — shopping, travel, work, and school. Examples include 店 (shop), 駅 (station), and 銀 (silver/money).

At this level, kanji start having more readings and appear more frequently in compound words. The jump from N5 to N4 is manageable, but you need to start paying attention to vocabulary in context rather than memorizing characters in isolation.

**Study tip:** Group kanji by theme (e.g., time, money, travel). Our [N4 vocabulary section](/n4/vocabulary/) organizes words by topic to reinforce learning.

## JLPT N3: 365 Kanji (612 cumulative)

N3 is where the difficulty accelerates significantly. With 365 new kanji (612 total), you encounter much more complex characters related to abstract concepts, society, and advanced daily life — such as 政策 (policy), 経済 (economy), and 環境 (environment).

Many learners find N3 the hardest jump because:
- Kanji have more strokes and readings
- Compounds become more common
- Reading passages test comprehension, not just recognition

**Study tip:** Use mnemonics and radical decomposition to remember complex kanji. For example, 意 (meaning/idea) combines 音 (sound) + 心 (heart) — the heart's sound is one's intention. Practice with [N3 flashcards](/n3/flashcards/) daily.

For a detailed breakdown of what each N3 kanji looks like and how to learn them, check our [N3 study guide](/blog/jlpt-n3-study-guide/) and [N3 study pages](/n3/study/).

## JLPT N2: 686 Kanji (1,298 cumulative)

N2 requires 686 additional kanji, totaling 1,298 characters. This is the level of newspaper reading, workplace communication, and serious adult content. Kanji at this level include 経営 (management), 交渉 (negotiation), and 監査 (audit).

N2 is the most commonly required level for Japanese university admissions and professional jobs. Passing N2 demonstrates you can handle real-world Japanese.

**Study tip:** Read actual Japanese materials — news articles, editorials, and essays. Use the [N2 practice tests](/n2/sets/1/) to build reading speed. For specific strategies, read our [JLPT N2 study guide](/blog/jlpt-n2-study-strategies/).

## JLPT N1: 1,237 Kanji (2,535 cumulative)

N1 is the highest JLPT level, requiring 1,237 new kanji (2,535 total). These are the characters used in literary works, academic papers, and professional Japanese. Examples include 薔薇 (rose), 憂鬱 (depression/melancholy), and 躊躇 (hesitation).

Many N1 kanji have obscure readings and meanings, and the exam tests them within complex vocabulary and long reading passages. Passing N1 typically requires 2,000+ hours of study.

**Study tip:** Go beyond textbooks. Read novels, academic articles, and Japanese news daily. Use [N1 vocabulary lists](/n1/vocabulary/) and [N1 flashcards](/n1/flashcards/) for focused study. Our [N1 preparation guide](/blog/jlpt-n1-preparation/) covers advanced strategies.
