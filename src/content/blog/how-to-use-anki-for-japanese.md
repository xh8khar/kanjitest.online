Anki is the most powerful spaced repetition system (SRS) available for language learning, and it is particularly well-suited for Japanese. The combination of highly customizable flashcards, community-shared decks, and advanced scheduling algorithms makes it an indispensable tool for learners at every level, from N5 beginners to N1 advanced students.

However, Anki's flexibility is both its greatest strength and its biggest weakness. Without proper configuration, beginners often end up with poorly designed cards, incorrect scheduling, and inefficient study habits. This guide covers everything you need to set up Anki for Japanese study from scratch, choose the right card types and add-ons, manage reviews efficiently, and optimize your learning using the latest FSRS algorithm.

## Why Use Anki for Japanese?

Japanese presents unique challenges that Anki addresses directly. Unlike European languages, Japanese has three writing systems (hiragana, katakana, and kanji), pitch accent, and a vocabulary system where one kanji can carry multiple readings depending on context. Traditional study methods struggle to handle this complexity efficiently.

Anki excels because it applies spaced repetition to each piece of information independently. A kanji you find difficult appears more frequently, while one you master quickly fades into the background. This targeted approach means you spend time exactly where it is most needed.

Research consistently shows that spaced repetition can triple retention rates compared to massed practice (cramming). For a long-term endeavor like learning Japanese, where reaching fluency requires thousands of hours, this efficiency gain is transformative.

If you are new to spaced repetition for Japanese, you may also want to read our guide on [how to use flashcards for kanji](/blog/flashcards-for-kanji/) for a broader overview of flashcard strategies beyond Anki.

## Getting Started with Anki

### Installation

Anki is available for Windows, macOS, Linux, Android (AnkiDroid, free), and iOS (AnkiMobile, paid). The desktop version is free on all platforms.

1. Download the desktop version from the official Anki website
2. Install and launch the application
3. Go to Tools > Add-ons to verify your installation is ready for customization

The mobile versions are essential for daily review. Most successful Japanese learners use Anki on their phone for quick sessions during commutes, breaks, or waiting in line.

### Creating Your First Deck

Organize your Japanese study into separate decks or use subdecks under a main Japanese deck:

- **Japanese::Kanji** — Individual kanji cards
- **Japanese::Vocabulary** — Word-level cards with example sentences
- **Japanese::Grammar** — Sentence cards demonstrating grammar patterns
- **Japanese::Sentences** — Full sentence cards for reading practice

The double-colon notation creates a hierarchy. A parent deck shows cards from all its subdecks combined, which is useful for daily review sessions.

## Choosing the Right Card Types

Anki's default card types are Basic, Basic (and reversed), and Cloze. Each serves a different purpose in Japanese study.

### Basic Card

A basic card has a front and a back. Use this for simple recall tasks:

Front: 食べる
Back: たべる (taberu) — to eat

This is ideal for vocabulary where you need to recall the reading and meaning from the kanji form.

### Basic (and Reversed)

This creates two cards: one asking for the reading from the kanji, and another asking for the kanji from the reading. This bidirectional approach is highly effective for Japanese because it strengthens both recognition and production.

For JLPT preparation, both directions are tested. The reading section requires kanji-to-meaning recognition, while the writing (if applicable) and vocabulary sections test the reverse direction.

### Cloze Deletion

Cloze cards hide a portion of a sentence, forcing you to recall the missing word. This is the most powerful card type for advanced learners because it tests vocabulary in context.

Front: 昨日、___で昼ご飯を食べました。(レストラン)
Back: 昨日、レストランで昼ご飯を食べました。

When you study with cloze cards, you internalize grammar patterns and word usage simultaneously. For this reason, many N2 and N1 learners use cloze cards almost exclusively.

### Custom Card Types for Japanese

Advanced Anki users create custom card templates tailored to Japanese. A well-designed Japanese card might show:

- **Front:** Kanji word + audio clip
- **Back:** Reading (furigana) + English meaning + example sentence + pitch accent notation + mnemonic

To create custom templates, you need to understand Anki's HTML and CSS-based card styling. Community-shared templates are available through AnkiWeb and the add-on ecosystem.

## Pre-Made Decks for Japanese

One of Anki's biggest advantages is the availability of shared decks created by experienced learners. Here are the most recommended decks for Japanese:

### JLPT Tango Decks

The JLPT Tango series (N5 through N1) contains sentence-based cards ordered by frequency. These decks are built around the i+1 principle: each new card introduces exactly one unknown element while the rest of the sentence is review. This makes them particularly effective for vocabulary acquisition in context.

### Kaishi 1.5

Kaishi is a modern, well-structured deck designed for beginners. It includes approximately 1,500 cards covering the most common Japanese words with native audio, example sentences, and pitch accent markers. The deck is actively maintained and optimized for the FSRS scheduler.

### Core 2k/6k/10k

The Core decks contain the most frequent Japanese words ranked by corpus frequency. The 2k deck covers the most common 2,000 words (enough for basic conversation), while the 6k and 10k versions extend to advanced vocabulary. These decks include native audio and example sentences.

### KanjiDamage

KanjiDamage is a mnemonic-based kanji deck that teaches kanji through radical decomposition stories. If you prefer the Heisig method, the KanjiDamage deck offers an alternative approach with more modern mnemonics.

### Custom Decks from Your Study Materials

The most effective decks are often the ones you build yourself from materials you encounter during study. When you read an article or watch a show and encounter a new word, adding it to your personal deck ensures it is immediately relevant and contextualized.

## Essential Add-Ons for Japanese

Anki's add-on ecosystem extends its functionality significantly. These are the most useful add-ons for Japanese learners.

### AJT Japanese (Anki Japanese Toolkit)

AJT Japanese is the most comprehensive add-on for Japanese study. It provides:

- Automatic furigana generation for any text
- Pitch accent display with color-coded notation
- Stroke order diagrams for kanji
- Automatic dictionary lookup from JMDict
- Audio generation using text-to-speech or pre-recorded audio

When you create a new card, AJT can automatically populate the reading, pitch accent pattern, and example sentences, saving hours of manual data entry.

### Migaku Japanese (formerly MorphMan)

Migaku Japanese offers advanced features for sentence mining and vocabulary management:

- Browser extension for mining words from Netflix, YouTube, and websites
- Automatic frequency filtering based on JLPT level
- Integration with Yomichan for one-click dictionary lookups
- Morphological analysis to track which words you have learned

For serious learners who consume Japanese media daily, Migaku transforms content consumption into structured study.

### AnkiConnect

AnkiConnect enables communication between Anki and external tools like Yomichan (browser popup dictionary) and mpvacious (video player integration). With AnkiConnect, you can:

- Look up a word in Yomichan and create an Anki card with one click
- Automatically capture audio and screenshots from videos
- Sync card creation across devices

### Review Heatmap

Review Heatmap adds a GitHub-style contribution graph to your Anki main screen. It shows your review activity over the past year, providing visual motivation to maintain your streak. Consistency is the single most important factor in SRS success, and the heatmap makes it easy to track.

## Configuring the FSRS Algorithm

Anki's original SM-2 scheduling algorithm served the community well for over a decade, but the newer FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler) represents a significant improvement.

### What is FSRS?

FSRS uses a machine learning model to predict the probability that you will recall a card on any given day. Instead of fixed intervals (1 day, 3 days, 7 days, etc.), FSRS dynamically calculates intervals based on the card's history and your overall performance patterns.

### Benefits of FSRS for Japanese

1. **Fewer reviews per day** — FSRS can reduce your daily review load by 20-30% while maintaining the same retention rate
2. **Better handling of leeches** — Cards you consistently struggle with are detected earlier and scheduled more appropriately
3. **Custom retention targets** — You can set different retention goals for different deck types (e.g., 90% for kanji, 85% for vocabulary)
4. **Optimal interval dispersion** — Reviews are spread more evenly, reducing the "burst" effect where hundreds of cards become due simultaneously

### How to Enable FSRS

1. Update Anki to version 23.10 or later
2. Go to Tools > Preferences > Scheduling
3. Enable FSRS and run the optimizer
4. Set your desired retention rate (90% is recommended for Japanese vocabulary)

After enabling FSRS, give it 2-3 weeks to calibrate. The algorithm learns from your review patterns, so its accuracy improves over time.

## Managing Daily Reviews

The most common reason learners abandon Anki is an overwhelming review backlog. Proper daily review management prevents this.

### Setting New Card Limits

The number of new cards you add per day directly determines your future review load. As a rule of thumb:

- **Light study (15 min/day):** 5 new cards per day
- **Moderate study (30 min/day):** 10-15 new cards per day
- **Intensive study (60 min/day):** 20-25 new cards per day

Each new card you learn today will generate approximately 0.5-1 review per day for the next month. Adding 20 new cards daily means roughly 400-600 reviews per month from new cards alone, plus reviews from older cards.

### Review Session Structure

An effective review session follows a three-phase structure:

**Phase 1: Due reviews (60% of time)**
Process all cards that are due for review. Answer honestly — do not give yourself a passing grade if you struggled. The algorithm depends on accurate self-assessment to schedule properly.

**Phase 2: New cards (30% of time)**
Learn new cards. For each card, read it carefully, create a mnemonic if needed, and test yourself once before moving to the next card.

**Phase 3: Review new cards (10% of time)**
After learning all new cards, go through them one more time. This immediate second pass significantly improves initial retention.

### Handling Backlogs

If you miss a few days of reviews, do not try to clear the entire backlog in one session. This leads to burnout and frustration. Instead:

1. Set a maximum daily review limit (e.g., 100 reviews per day)
2. Let FSRS handle the rescheduling — the algorithm will redistribute overdue cards
3. Do not add new cards until the backlog is under control
4. Consider reducing new card intake going forward

### When to Suspend or Delete Cards

Not every card is worth keeping. Suspend or delete cards that:

- You have seen 10+ times but still cannot recall
- Contain words you never encounter in real Japanese materials
- Are too easy (you answer correctly within 1 second every time)
- Test information you no longer need

A lean, focused deck is more effective than a bloated one.

## Integrating Anki with Other Study Methods

Anki is most effective when combined with other learning activities rather than used in isolation.

### Before Reading

Preview the vocabulary for an article or lesson in Anki before reading. This primes your brain for the new words and reduces the cognitive load during reading.

### After Reading

Extract unknown words from the text and create Anki cards for them. This ensures every card you study is contextualized and relevant.

### Combining with Pitch Accent Study

Japanese pitch accent significantly affects comprehensibility. When you create vocabulary cards, include the pitch accent pattern (using AJT or Migaku) and practice pronouncing the word aloud during review.

For a detailed explanation of pitch accent and why it matters, read our guide on [Japanese pitch accent](/blog/japanese-pitch-accent/).

### Pairing with Grammar Study

Grammar patterns are best learned through example sentences rather than isolated rules. Create cloze-deletion cards from sentences in your grammar textbook. Each card tests one grammar point in a natural context.

Our guide on [essential Japanese grammar](/blog/essential-japanese-grammar/) covers the grammar patterns you should incorporate into your Anki sentence cards.

### Using with Kanji Radicals

When creating kanji cards, include the radical breakdown on the back. This connects each kanji to its components and helps you recognize patterns across different characters.

For a complete reference on radicals, see our [kanji radicals guide](/blog/kanji-radicals-guide/).

## Advanced Anki Workflows

### Sentence Mining

Sentence mining is the practice of extracting sentences from native media (anime, drama, news, books) and turning them into Anki cards. This is the most effective advanced workflow for reaching N2 and N1 levels.

Workflow:
1. Watch a show or read an article
2. When you encounter an unknown word, pause and look it up
3. Capture the sentence containing the word (using Migaku or mpvacious)
4. Create an Anki card with the sentence, word reading, and meaning
5. Add audio of the sentence if available

Over months of consistent mining, you build a deck of thousands of sentences drawn from authentic materials, all personally relevant to you.

### Position-Dependent Cards

For vocabulary, create position-dependent cards that test the same word in different contexts. A single word might appear in three different cards, each with a different sentence. This prevents context-dependent memorization (where you remember the word only in one specific sentence).

### Shared Decks as Raw Material

Instead of using shared decks directly as study material, use them as a source of cards to import into your personal deck after filtering. This gives you control over which words you learn and in what order.

## Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

### Pitfall 1: Adding Too Many Cards Too Quickly

The enthusiasm of a new Anki user often leads to adding hundreds of cards in the first week. Within a month, the review load becomes unmanageable. Start with 5-10 new cards per day and only increase after establishing a consistent review habit.

### Pitfall 2: Ignoring Audio

Many learners create reading-only cards without audio. This leads to weak pronunciation and difficulty understanding spoken Japanese. Always include native audio on your cards.

### Pitfall 3: Using Overly Complex Cards

A card should test exactly one piece of information. A card that asks you to recall the reading, meaning, stroke order, and a compound word is ineffective because you cannot isolate which part you struggled with.

### Pitfall 4: Not Reviewing Daily

Skipping a day occasionally is fine, but more than 2-3 missed days disrupts the spacing algorithm. Anki works best as a daily habit, not an occasional study session.

### Pitfall 5: Never Reviewing Card Design

As you advance, your card design should evolve. N5 kanji cards look very different from N1 sentence cards. Periodically review your card templates and update them to match your current level.

## Measuring Your Progress

Anki provides several statistics to track your progress:

- **Review count:** Total cards reviewed today
- **Average time per card:** Should decrease as you get faster
- **Retention rate:** Percentage of cards answered correctly
- **Mature card retention:** Retention rate for cards seen 21+ days ago (target: 85-95%)
- **Card count:** Total number of cards in your collection

Aim for a mature card retention rate of 85-90%. If it drops below 80%, your new card intake is too high or your card design needs improvement.

## Sample Daily Anki Routine

**Morning (10-15 minutes):**
- Process all due reviews from the Japanese main deck
- Focus on speed — each card should take 3-5 seconds

**Lunch (5 minutes):**
- Review 5-10 mature cards from your vocabulary subdeck
- Practice pronouncing each word aloud

**Evening (15-20 minutes):**
- Learn 10 new cards (phased approach: learn, then immediate review)
- Mine 2-3 sentences from today's reading or listening practice
- Add new cards to your personal deck

This routine requires 30-40 minutes total and yields steady progress of 300 new words per month.

## Final Thoughts

Anki is not a magic solution — it is a tool that rewards consistent, thoughtful use. The learners who succeed with Anki are not necessarily the ones who use the most add-ons or the most complex card templates. They are the ones who show up every day, answer honestly, and continuously refine their approach.

Start simple. Use the default card types, download one well-reviewed shared deck, and commit to 10 minutes per day for the first week. Once the habit is established, explore add-ons and custom templates. The best Anki setup is one that you will actually use consistently.
