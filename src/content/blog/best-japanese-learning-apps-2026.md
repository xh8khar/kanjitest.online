This guide reviews the best free Japanese learning apps available in 2026, focusing on tools that actually help you pass the JLPT. We cover kanji study, vocabulary building, grammar practice, reading practice, and listening comprehension.

## 2. Anki — Best Spaced Repetition System

**Free (desktop, Android) | $25 (iOS) | Desktop & Mobile**

Anki remains the gold standard for spaced repetition in 2026. The introduction of the FSRS algorithm made it even more efficient, reducing daily review loads by 20-30% while maintaining retention.

### Key Features

- Advanced spaced repetition with FSRS algorithm
- Highly customizable card templates
- Community-created decks for JLPT (Tango, Kaishi, Core 2k/6k)
- Add-ons for automated card creation, pitch accent, and furigana

### Best For

Learners who want algorithm-optimized long-term memorization and are willing to invest time in setup. Read our [detailed guide on using Anki for Japanese](/blog/how-to-use-anki-for-japanese/) for setup instructions.

## 3. Duolingo — Best for Beginners (Limited for JLPT)

**Free (with ads) | iOS, Android, Web**

Duolingo's Japanese course has improved significantly, adding more kanji content and better audio. It is excellent for absolute beginners learning hiragana, katakana, and basic phrases.

### Limitations for JLPT

Duolingo does not organize content by JLPT levels. It teaches useful daily conversation but does not systematically cover the kanji and vocabulary required for JLPT N5 through N1. Use it as a supplementary tool for listening practice and basic grammar exposure, but do not rely on it as your primary JLPT prep tool.

## 4. Bunpro — Best Grammar SRS

**Free tier available | Paid subscription | Web**

Bunpro is a dedicated Japanese grammar study tool that uses spaced repetition specifically for grammar points. It covers grammar from N5 through N1, organized by JLPT level.

### Key Features

- Grammar-specific SRS with custom scheduling
- Explanations from multiple textbooks (Genki, Minna no Nihongo, Tobira)
- Example sentences with readings
- Built-in review tracking

### Best For

Learners who struggle with grammar retention and want a dedicated SRS system for grammar points. Bunpro complements kanji-focused tools like KanjiTest.Online perfectly.

## 5. Jisho & Takoboto — Best Dictionaries

**Free | Web & Mobile**

Jisho.org and Takoboto are essential Japanese-English dictionaries. Jisho is more comprehensive on the web, while Takoboto offers a better mobile experience with offline support.

### Key Features

- Kanji radical search and stroke order
- Example sentences from native sources
- Word frequency indicators
- JLPT level tags

### Best For

Quick lookups, checking readings of unfamiliar kanji, and finding example sentences. Every serious learner should have at least one Japanese dictionary app installed.

## 6. NHK News Easy — Best Reading Practice

**Free | Web & Mobile App**

NHK News Easy provides simplified news articles written in easy Japanese with:
- Furigana for all kanji
- Simplified vocabulary
- Audio narration by native speakers
- Illustrations to aid comprehension

### Best For

Moving from textbook Japanese to real-world reading. N3 and above learners should read at least one NHK Easy article daily. For N5 and N4 learners, start with the [N5 vocabulary](/n5/vocabulary/) and [study pages](/n5/study/) to build foundational kanji knowledge before tackling news articles.

## 7. Rikaitan (formerly Yomichan) — Best Browser Extension

**Free | Browser Extension**

Rikaitan is a browser-based Japanese dictionary that provides instant popup definitions when you hover over Japanese text on any website.

### Key Features

- One-click definitions with readings
- Frequency markers
- Anki integration for card creation
- Custom dictionary support

### Best For

Reading Japanese websites, news, and blogs. Combined with Anki, it creates a powerful reading-to-SRS pipeline.
